About

Researcher who ships. Engineer who reads papers.

Applied AI engineer building computer-vision systems for production. Four years at TOHU MEDIA, a Māori-owned media-production company in Auckland (full-time since June 2025), designing and deploying object-detection and OCR pipelines on real film-industry video. Specific deliverables are under NDA. In parallel I’m finishing a PhD at AUT on deep learning for video understanding. First-author paper accepted at Pattern Recognition (Q1, 2026); six papers and 70+ Google Scholar citations so far.

The research and the production work keep each other honest. When a new CV paper claims state of the art, I usually have a good sense of whether it’ll survive contact with messy real video or whether it’s a benchmark trick. When something I’ve shipped breaks in production, I can usually trace the failure back to a modelling assumption that didn’t hold up. I take systems all the way through: dataset spec, PyTorch training, Docker-packaged inference on cloud GPU servers, and the back-and-forth with the editors and creative director who actually use the output.

Before the PhD I taught computer science to engineering undergraduates at a Chinese university for four years (programming, Java, IoT, RFID), then co-founded a small private training school for two years. I divested cleanly in 2021 and moved to New Zealand. That period left me with two things most engineers don’t have: a lot of practice explaining technical work to non-technical people, and the experience of running an actual business with a P&L. Writing code is one part of shipping; it isn’t the whole thing.

On top of the CV work, I’ve also been building agentic systems on LLM APIs (Claude and GPT integration, tool use, skill-based agents). My read is that the next chapter of computer-vision deployment isn’t smarter predictions — it’s models that act on what they see.

“Models live in the lab. Systems live in the world. I want to build the second one.” — guiding line for the next phase
